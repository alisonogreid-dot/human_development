The Human Development Index (HDI)

The HDI combines three dimensions to give a fuller picture of how "developed" a country is — measured as a score between 0 and 1. It was created because GDP alone doesn't tell you how people are actually living.

❤️
Long & Healthy Life
Measured by: Life expectancy at birth
📚
Knowledge
Measured by: Average + expected years of schooling
🏠
A Decent Standard of Living
Measured by: GNI per capita (PPP $)
↓ These three combine into…
Human Development Index (HDI)
A score between 0 and 1 — the closer to 1, the higher the human development

Vocabulary

Click any term to expand its definition. Use the filters to focus on one category at a time.

Elements that make up the level of production in a country's economy — things like income and production.
💡 Think of these as the "money side" of development — how much a country earns and produces.
The total amount of money earned by a nation's people and businesses. It includes GDP (everything produced inside the country) plus income received from overseas.
💡 GNI is like a country's total salary — including money its citizens earn abroad.
GNI divided by the country's population. This gives a comparable wealth rate — how much, on average, each person "earns."
💡 Per capita = "per person." It makes it possible to compare a huge country like India to a small one like Norway.
An adjustment to GDP per capita that accounts for the fact that $1 buys more (or less) depending on where you are. It makes income comparisons fairer across countries.
💡 A coffee in Oslo might cost $6, but in Hanoi it might cost $1. PPP adjusts for that difference so we can compare incomes more accurately.
Elements that tell us about how people are doing socially — primarily health and education.
💡 Think of these as the "people side" of development — how long and how well people actually live.
Birth rates and death rates, especially tracked over time. These show us how healthy and safe a society is — and where it's heading demographically.
A metric that summarises death rates across all age groups in one year, to estimate how long people can expect to live. It's one of the HDI's three pillars — representing a "long and healthy life."
💡 Life expectancy doesn't predict how long you will live — it captures how safe and healthy a society is right now.
Broader measures like life expectancy, infant mortality, and education rates. Together, these show how people's lives are shaped from birth to death.
The HDI measures two things for education: the average number of years of schooling adults have completed, and the expected years of schooling a child entering school can look forward to.
A single number (between 0 and 1) that combines economic and social factors to give a fuller picture of how "developed" a country is. Created by the UNDP because GDP alone doesn't capture quality of life.
💡 Norway's HDI is ~0.96. Central African Republic's is ~0.40. But what do those numbers hide? That's a question we'll keep returning to.
In the HDI, "standard of living" is measured by GNI per capita — how much money, on average, a person earns in a year. It's a proxy for whether people can afford a decent life.
💡 Notice that HDI uses money (GNI) as a stand-in for "decent life." Is money the best way to measure that? Something to think about…
A country with lower average incomes, often lower rates of education, lower life expectancies, and an economy focused on raw materials and agriculture rather than finished products.
⚠️ Labels like LEDC are useful shortcuts, but they can flatten the complexity of a country's situation. Keep that tension in mind.
A country with higher average incomes, high rates of education and life expectancy. Typically produces finished products using raw materials imported from LEDCs.
⚠️ Norway is an MEDC — but does that mean everything here is "developed"? Are there communities or groups within MEDCs who experience LEDC-like conditions?

Your Country Report

Work in your group to produce a country report (slides or an infographic) that helps answer the inquiry question: What does it mean to be a "developed" country?

You can use PowerPoint, Canva, or any format that lets you present data clearly. See the example on Greece for layout ideas.

1

Context & History

Give a brief overview of your country's history of development and its situation within its region. What's the story?

2

Government & People

What type of government does your country have? How much influence do ordinary people have in their government and daily lives?

3

The HDI Pillars

Cover each of the three dimensions with data:

❤️ Life Expectancy — include infant mortality or other health indicators if you have time

📚 Education — average and expected years of schooling

🏠 Standard of Living — GNI or GDP per capita

4

HDI Score & Your Analysis

What is your country's HDI? Based on everything you've found — how likely would you want to live there? Use the data to support your reasoning.
